About the Spot Gold Price

The spot price of gold reflects the real‑time value of gold on global commodities exchanges. It is the basis for pricing gold jewelry, coins, and bullion and serves as the benchmark for gold buyers and sellers worldwide.

Gold prices are influenced by a wide range of economic and geopolitical factors including inflation, currency strength, interest rates, and global supply and demand. As a safe‑haven asset, gold often increases in value during periods of market uncertainty or global instability.

The spot price is typically quoted in troy ounces, but can also be calculated per gram or kilogram depending on the region or purpose. Most dealers quote gold in U.S. dollars, but conversions to other currencies are common for international buyers.

Understanding the spot price helps consumers and investors make informed decisions when buying or selling gold. It also helps explain why offers are made below spot— to account for refining, handling, risk, and resale margins.

Acerca del Precio Spot del Oro

El precio spot del oro refleja el valor en tiempo real del oro en los mercados mundiales de materias primas. Es la base para fijar el precio de joyas, monedas y lingotes de oro, y sirve como referencia para compradores y vendedores de oro en todo el mundo.

Los precios del oro están influenciados por una amplia gama de factores económicos y geopolíticos, incluyendo la inflación, la fortaleza de la moneda, las tasas de interés y la oferta y demanda global. Como activo refugio, el oro a menudo aumenta su valor durante períodos de incertidumbre en el mercado o inestabilidad global.

El precio spot generalmente se cotiza en onzas troy, pero también puede calcularse por gramo o kilogramo dependiendo de la región o propósito. La mayoría de los distribuidores cotizan el oro en dólares estadounidenses, pero las conversiones a otras monedas son comunes.

Entender el precio spot ayuda a los consumidores e inversores a tomar decisiones informadas al comprar o vender oro. También ayuda a explicar por qué las ofertas se hacen por debajo del precio spot— para tener en cuenta los costos de refinación, manipulación, riesgo y márgenes de reventa.

Gold in the Law

Is there sales tax on gold?
Not in our home state of Arizona. Therefore, purchases made through our service are not subject to sales tax.

Are gold bullion coins legal tender?
Yes — most gold bullion coins carry a nominal face value, allowing them to cross borders without being taxed or treated like raw bullion.

Do bullion coins come with a certificate of authenticity?
Yes. The mint or refiner stamps each piece with its weight, purity, and origin — essentially making the gold itself the certificate. Gold's unique physical properties also make authenticity easy to verify using tests that date back to ancient times.

Are there counterfeit gold coins?
While counterfeits exist, gold's density and weight make it very difficult to fake convincingly. We always recommend working with trusted dealers to ensure authenticity.

Was gold ownership ever illegal in the U.S.?
Yes — from 1933 to 1974, Americans couldn't legally own gold bullion without special licenses. These restrictions ended on January 1, 1975.

When were gold restrictions lifted?
As of January 1, 1975, private citizens were again allowed to own any form or quantity of gold freely and without licenses.

Are there limits on how much gold I can own?
No — there are no federal limits. Your gold ownership is only limited by your personal budget.

Do you report my gold purchases?
Absolutely not. Your transactions with us are private. We are under no obligation to report any gold purchases to any government agency.

What about purchases over $10,000?
Reporting is only triggered when transactions involve actual cash (like large sums of currency). Bank wires or personal checks — regardless of size — are not reported.

Do you report gold sales?
Only in very specific cases involving 25+ ounce sales of certain bullion coins (like Krugerrands, Maple Leafs, or Onzas). In those cases, a 1099‑B form may be filed.

Do I have to report my gold purchases?
No. There is no federal, state, or local agency that tracks or monitors how much gold you own.

Are profits from selling gold taxable?
Yes — just like any investment, gains on gold are subject to capital‑gains taxes if you sell for a profit.
